Is this an emergency? A guide for people who Google their dog's symptoms at 2am
The vet industry is designed to make you feel guilty for going too much and terrified about not going enough. Two founders explain why that is the system’s fault, not yours.
Partnership By Marcus Webb, founder of Zoomies, and Lorna Winter, co-founder of Zigzag
📸
A dog (cockapoo or rescue) on a sofa in a warmly lit London flat. Phone nearby, screen faintly glowing. Late night. Slightly cluttered coffee table. The dog looks completely unbothered. Real Tuesday, not a photoshoot.
There is a 94% chance the dog is fine. There is a 0% chance you will be able to sleep until you are sure.

It is 2am. Humphrey has sneezed three times. The third sneeze sounded different. You cannot explain how, but you noticed.

You open your phone. You type “dog sneezing at night.” The results are fine. Probably nothing, could be dust, here is a forum thread from 2017 that is mostly people arguing about something unrelated. You close the tab. You open it again. You type “dog sneezing at night causes.” You read four results. One of them has a subheading that says “When to worry” and you know, you absolutely know, that you should stop reading, and you do not stop reading.

Twenty-three minutes later you are deep in something called nasal turbinate disease and Humphrey is fast asleep on the good cushion, completely unbothered. You will not sleep for another hour. Possibly two.

Right. We need to talk about this.

Not in a “here’s what you should do differently” way. In a “this is not your fault and we can explain exactly whose fault it is” way. Because the 2am spiral is not neurosis. It is not you being one of those dog owners, the ones the internet makes fun of, the ones who refer to their dog as their child and who, yes, fine, absolutely do refer to their dog as their child and would like everyone to please mind their own business. The spiral happens because you love something that cannot tell you what is wrong, and the system that is supposed to help (the vet, the advice, the whole infrastructure of professional support) has been built in a way that makes accessing it feel like a decision that needs to be justified. Financially. Emotionally. To yourself, at 2am, alone, with your phone.

That is the system. Not you.

Marcus built Zoomies because he got properly annoyed watching an industry extract money from people in their most worried moments and call it healthcare. Lorna built Zigzag because she spent years watching new puppy owners arrive with love, confidence, and a completely inadequate understanding of what the first twelve weeks were actually going to feel like. Between us, we have watched a lot of people have a lot of bad nights that did not need to happen. This is for them. It is for you, Humphrey’s mum, specifically.

Nobody tells you what is actually coming in the first twelve weeks. Not the sanitised version. The real version.

Those weeks are not the hard bit you get through to reach the easy bit. They are the bit that decides almost everything else. The way Humphrey responds to strangers, to other dogs, to the postman’s knock, to fireworks in November. All of it is getting set right now, while you are running on four hours of sleep and Googling whether it is normal for a puppy to eat that much of the skirting board. The next couple of years you will be living with what these weeks produce, which is either a dog who thinks the world is broadly fine, or one who doesn’t, and it is heartbreaking how often the difference comes down to whether someone gave the owner the right information at the right time.

The puppy training industry has not historically been interested in giving it. There are books that contradict each other on points they both present as settled. There are YouTube channels where the dog is flawless and the owner is serene and everyone has a treat pouch that fits their aesthetic. There is a woman in every park who will tell you her dog was fully trained in a week, and look, maybe that is true, or maybe her definition of “trained” has some flexibility to it. And then there is your Tuesday morning, with your actual dog, who understood “sit” perfectly last Thursday and has apparently no memory of it whatsoever, and is now systematically excavating the back garden for reasons that remain unclear.

Zigzag exists because this kept happening. People who wanted to get it right, who genuinely tried, who still ended up six months in with an anxious dog and a vague sense they had missed something important in week four. The app gives you daily lessons built around your specific dog, their breed, their age, the stage they are in right now. It tells you what this week looks like and what to expect from the next one. There are qualified trainers available around the clock via in-app chat, because Humphrey does not limit his more interesting behaviour to business hours.

The most popular module, the one that more people engage with than anything else on the whole app, is noise socialisation. Teaching puppies not to be frightened of fireworks. Doorbells. The world.

When we tell people this they always laugh, and then go quiet for a second. Of course it is. We are a city of people who check the dates in October to see how close the displays are to our postcode. We lie awake in early November doing the maths on whether it is worth driving somewhere quieter. We are fully grown adults who have, at least once, Googled “how to comfort dog during fireworks” with the focus and urgency of someone defusing something.

“We are a city of people who check the dates in October to see how close the fireworks displays are to our postcode.”

“The early weeks are when everything gets set,” says Lorna. “The way a dog responds to the world, the habits an owner builds, whether they feel supported or completely at sea with it. We see what happens when people have the right help early. It changes everything.”

A million people have downloaded the app. Revenue went up 264% last year. That is not a marketing number. That is a lot of people on a lot of Tuesdays with nowhere useful to put their questions.

Now. The vet problem. This one makes Marcus angry and honestly it makes us both angry and we think it should make you angry too.

A standard vet visit in the UK costs between £50 and £80. That is before treatment, before a diagnosis, before the thing you came in for has started. Then whatever they find costs extra. Vet costs have risen roughly 60% over eight years, and pet insurance premiums went up over 5% above March 2024 levels by early this year.

The behaviour this produces is predictable, understandable, and bad for dogs. You do the maths. You decide the sneeze is probably not worth eighty quid. You wait. You watch. You Google at 2am instead of just going, because going has a price attached and you are not sure the sneeze has crossed the threshold yet.

Sometimes you are right and it was nothing. Sometimes you wait too long. Sometimes you come out of the appointment having spent £200 on something that would have cost £80 two weeks ago. Then you feel guilty for waiting, while Humphrey looks at you with an expression that is hard to read.

Marcus built Zoomies because this made him angry. Actually angry. The pay-per-visit model creates an invisible barrier between you and the person who can tell you whether the sneeze is fine, and the height of that barrier goes up every time you open your banking app. Small things become bigger things. Owners are quietly, constantly making a financial calculation that has nothing to do with whether their dog needs help.

“Traditionally at a vets, you visit for a problem, and quite often, see the bills rack up,” he says. “Things like x-rays, travel documents, neutering, these cost hundreds of pounds. The worry of those costs leads some owners to just avoid treatment altogether. We wanted to create something where you can come in whenever you want, and you won’t get another bill for a visit. Ever.”

“Going when you are not sure is the point. That is the reason the system was supposed to support all along, and mostly hasn’t.”

Zoomies is a membership. Thirty-three pounds a month. For that you get unlimited consultations, all vaccinations including rabies, annual dental cleaning (worth upwards of £500 a year at standard rates, take a moment with that), diagnostics, microchipping, and neutering. There is a WhatsApp triage, which means that when Humphrey does the paw thing at 7am on a Wednesday, you take a photo, send it, and within minutes someone tells you either come in or here is why it is fine. No spiral. No nasal turbinate disease. No convincing yourself it is probably nothing while also not quite believing it is probably nothing.

“The WhatsApp thing is what sold me,” one member said. “Sent a photo of Milo’s paw at 7am, got advice straight away, had an appointment that afternoon. It’s like having a vet on speed dial.” Another just said: “Such a find. This is how all vets should be.” Which is either the loveliest compliment or the most quietly devastating verdict on the industry, and we suspect it is both.

The NPS is over 70. Retention is, in Marcus’s words, Netflix-level. People are staying. More importantly, people are going when they are not sure. That is the entire point. Going when you are not sure, because Humphrey is the one who needs looking at and not your bank balance, is how you catch things early. It is how you build a relationship with a vet who knows him well enough to notice when something is different. It is how you stop doing the maths at 2am.

So. What does it actually look like when both of these things are working.

You have an app that tells you what this week looks like developmentally, what to expect from the next one, and the one after that. It told you about the socialisation window before it closed. It told you about fireworks before November arrived. There is a trainer in your pocket at 11pm when the question comes that you are too embarrassed to put in a forum. The first twelve weeks have a shape. They are still hard, because Humphrey is a force of nature with the impulse control of a golden retriever puppy, which is to say none, but hard-with-a-shape is a different experience to hard-without-one.

And when the 2am sneeze happens, you send a WhatsApp. You get an answer. If the answer is come in, you come in, because there is no threshold to clear anymore, no maths to do in the waiting room. Humphrey is seen by someone who has been seeing him since the beginning, who knows his patterns, who will notice when something is actually off.

The 2am thing is not going away entirely. It is, at some level, just what it costs to love something that cannot tell you when it is hurting. You accepted that when Humphrey arrived, along with the destroyed skirting board and the cushion situation and the specific experience of standing in the park at 6:45am shouting “HUMPHREY, COME HERE, HUMPHREY” while Humphrey makes sustained eye contact with you from twelve feet away and refuses to move.

There is a version where the 2am ends at the first search. Where you put the phone down because there is someone sensible to ask in the morning, and the morning has a vet who knows Humphrey’s name and is not going to charge you for the asking. Where the first year had a shape, and you came out of it with a dog who thinks the world is roughly fine, and an owner who has stopped dreading November.

It is the same animal, from two different ends. And it is available now.

And Humphrey’s sneeze was almost certainly nothing. Get some sleep.
