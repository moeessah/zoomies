Growth Strategy · Channel by Channel
How I'd grow Zoomies: six channels, prioritised, with kill thresholds.

The order matters: attribution, referral, local, paid, SEO, then US. Each channel gets a number and a kill point.

01
Foundation · Month 1
Fix attribution before spending another pound
Right now Zoomies is spending on paid, running a referral programme, and doing local activity across six clinics. Without clean attribution, you don't know which of those is actually driving sign-ups. UTM taxonomy standardised across every campaign. Pixel audit. GA4 and CRM connected so member LTV ties back to acquisition source from day one. Unique referral code per member at sign-up. This comes first because every channel decision that follows depends on trusting the numbers.
Deliverable: A clinic-by-clinic growth dashboard. CAC by channel, payback by cohort, LTV by acquisition source.
02
Activate · Month 1–2
The referral programme just launched. Now make it work.
It launched but it hasn't been activated. 6,000 members, 40% acquired through word of mouth, and most of those advocates have never been asked to share with a tool that actually works. Pull the top 500 members by visit frequency and NPS signal. Give them a reason to share at the moment they're most likely to. Double-sided incentive, two variants in test. Every share tracked back to a member, a cohort, and an acquisition source. Full design below.
Hypothesis: 15–20% activation rate in 90 days. Kill if: under 8% at day 60. Test new incentive structure before putting more behind it.
03
Test · Month 2–3
Own the streets, not just the feed
Zoomies has a physical business, which means it can do things digital-only competitors can't. Vet nurse pop-ups at Pets at Home on Saturday mornings. Someone from the clinic at every dog park, groomer, and independent pet shop within a mile. QR codes on all physical materials linking to clinic-specific landing pages so every conversion is tracked back to a location. Test Islington first. Measure footfall-to-member rate before rolling across all six clinics.
Test: Islington first. Each clinic gets its own UTM-tracked page. Measure trial-to-member conversion before expanding.
04
Scale · Month 2–4
Paid media: four creatives, one winner
Four creative angles at £50/day each: member story UGC, vet-bill comparison static, "vet explains" short video, direct offer ad. Whichever returns CAC under £85 with a positive 30-day LTV signal earns the budget. At 85% monthly retention, 12-month LTV is roughly £290, so £85 CAC is a 3.5-month payback. UGC consistently outperforms polished brand creative in this category, so that gets tested first while the others run in parallel.
Kill: CAC over £120 at 30 days. Scale: sub-£85 with positive LTV cohort signal.
05
Build · Month 1–6
SEO and AEO for demand that already exists
London has 2.1M+ pet-owning households. They're searching vet cost questions every day. Build for what they're already looking for: "how much does a vet check-up cost London," "is pet insurance worth it," "cat vaccine cost UK." The same content strategy that ranks on Google also needs to show up when someone asks ChatGPT or Perplexity the same question. That requires vet-authored answers with clinical authority, which is something no content farm can produce. Full AEO strategy below.
Target: 5,000 organic sessions/month by month 9. Monthly LLM citation check across 10 queries. 10 articles live by month 3.
06
US Launch · Now
Clifton NJ: own the zip before the city
07011–07013 first. Nextdoor ads to dog-owner households in a 2-mile radius. Sponsor the three biggest local pet Facebook groups. Every groomer, trainer, and dog daycare within 3 miles gets a personal visit from someone at Zoomies, not a flyer in the post. Each partner gets a unique tracked URL so sign-ups are attributed by relationship, not campaign. Banfield charges comparable prices and delivers a fraction of the care. That gap is the whole campaign.
Measure: sign-ups per partner URL. Return to the ones converting, drop the ones that aren't. If the numbers hold, add the next ZIP code.

Banfield's Wellness Plans run $35–55/month depending on tier and pet type. You get a fixed annual checklist, one or two exams, core vaccines, maybe a dental cleaning. 15-minute appointments. Rotating staff who don't know your dog's name.

Zoomies at $55 is unlimited care, the same vet every time, remote triage included. Banfield's model is built around limiting visits. Zoomies' is built around the relationship. They haven't built a product people actually want to tell their friends about.

Why unique URLs per partner matter

When a groomer sends a customer to zoomies.com/clifton-paws and they sign up, you know the groomer drove that conversion. You know the relationship is worth maintaining. You know what to offer them to keep referring. Without it, partner spend is a guess.

Search Strategy · SEO + AEO
Google is one channel. LLMs are another.

Traditional SEO and Answer Engine Optimisation are related but they're not the same job. Google ranks the page that best satisfies search intent. ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini cite the source that most authoritatively answers the question. Those are different targets and they need different strategies.

Right now, if you ask Perplexity "is a vet membership worth it in the UK," Zoomies doesn't appear. The sources that do appear: VetsNow, Vets4Pets, a few insurance comparison sites. Those are the publications to target for press, partnerships, and backlinks.

For SEO: build utility content around the vet cost queries London pet owners are already searching. "How much does a vet check-up cost in London." "Is pet insurance worth it UK." "Cat vaccine cost 2025." Target 50 pieces live by month 6. The content examples in this application are the starting point.

For AEO: the advantage is clinical authority. A vet writing "here's what you're actually paying for in a London vet consultation" gets cited by the publications LLMs draw from. A growth marketer's blog post doesn't. FAQ schema, structured data, concise answers embedded inside longer pieces. The Guardian article in this application is already doing this. The job is to produce more like it.

High-intent queries to own: both channels

🔍 "vet membership London" SEO · high intent

Why this is hard to copy

Any growth hire can commission content. What they can't commission is a vet's clinical authority. Every piece Zoomies publishes that's written or reviewed by a vet carries a trust signal that third-party publications want to cite, and that LLMs weight when assembling answers.

Measurement cadence: once a month, run 10 high-intent queries through ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini. Track whether Zoomies appears. If not, note which sources are cited instead. Those are the publications to target for press and partnerships.

K-factor: the compounding case for referral

Work backwards from what already exists. 6,000 members, 40% from word of mouth. Roughly 2,400 members acquired through WOM with no incentive, no share tool, no referral infrastructure. The implied organic referral coefficient is somewhere around 0.4: every 10 members organically referred 4 more.

That's a strong foundation. Most consumer products would spend six figures in paid to reach an organic K of 0.2. Zoomies got there by having a product people actually talk about.

K at 0.6 instead of 0.4 adds roughly 1,800 members by month 18, about £59,000/month additional revenue at £33/month.

How the referral engine works: full design

Zoomies has one structural advantage over digital-only referral programmes: a physical clinic. When a member has just had a good appointment, a vet nurse can ask them in person, hand them a card, and generate their personal share link on the spot. No email sequence can replicate that timing. The design below is built around that moment, then extends into email for members who don't come in.

1 month free

What the friend gets
First month free

1
Identify the advocates
Pull members with 3+ visits in 6 months. Cross-reference NPS data. The top 500 are the activation cohort. They already love Zoomies, they just haven't been given a tool. This isn't a broadcast email to 6,000 members. It's targeted outreach to the 500 people most likely to share.
2
The right moment
Three triggers in priority order. First: in-clinic, at the exit. A vet nurse asks "have you told any dog-parent friends about us?" and hands a card with a QR that generates the member's personal link on the spot. They just had a good experience. That's when people share, not two days later when an email lands.
Second: email 48 hours after first appointment. Third: email 72 hours after sign-up. The in-clinic ask is the highest-conversion trigger because it's physical and immediate. The emails catch members who don't come in regularly.
3
Shareable materials
Unique code per member. WhatsApp share text pre-written to sound like a person texting, not a referral programme. Instagram story template. Physical card for in-clinic handout. Every format, because members share differently. One person sends a text. Another posts a story. Another hands a card to someone at the dog park.
4
Close the loop
Track referrals sent, referrals activated, and referred member LTV vs organic LTV as a separate cohort from day one. Tag every referred member in the CRM at acquisition. If referred members retain longer, that changes what the programme can spend on incentives and gives referral a stronger claim on the budget.

Variant A: 1 month free for referrer + 1 month free for friend. Clean, symmetric, easy to explain.

Variant B: £25 credit for referrer + first visit free for friend. Variant B feels like giving a friend a useful first visit, rather than collecting a reward. Worth testing because if it converts higher, it tells you something specific about what Zoomies members actually care about.

Variant A: email 72 hours after sign-up. "Know another dog parent who would use this?" Catches the excitement before it settles.

Variant B: email 48 hours after first appointment. "We loved meeting [dog name]. Here's something for your dog-parent friends." Sent after trust has been earned through experience, not just promised at sign-up.

The hypothesis is that Variant B wins. People share things they've actually been through. Measure share rate, activation rate, and 90-day LTV of the friend cohort. Let the cohort data settle it.

Messaging Framework
Three audiences. Three different reasons to join.
The Anxiety Spiral
Millennial, first pet, London, 28–38. Avoids the vet because the unknown bill is scarier than the symptom.
Their fear
I don't know what it will cost before I go in. So I put it off. Then I put it off more.
"Stop doing the maths before every appointment."
The proof
A third of UK pet owners have delayed a vet visit because of cost. Zoomies removes that equation. One number every month, everything included. You go when you need to, not when you've saved up for it.
Channel first
Instagram, TikTok, Google high-intent cost queries, Nextdoor. Any context where the moment of anxiety is recent.
The Frustrated Optimiser
Late 30s, already has insurance, still anxious. Pays for premium options and still feels like the vet is expensive.
Their frustration
I have insurance and I still pay consultation fees. My dog sees a different vet every time. The claim takes three weeks.
"Your vet. Not whoever the insurer approves."
The proof
NPS over 70. Members visit 3.2 times per year on average, far above the national rate, because when the bill disappears, people actually go. Same vet, same clinic, every time. That relationship is the product.
Channel first
Google ("is pet insurance worth it"), LinkedIn, press, email partnerships with insurance comparison sites.
The Multi-Pet Household
Two or three pets. Spending £150–200/month across separate vet appointments, separate consultation fees, separate everything. At £33 per pet per month, Zoomies >>> .
Their reality
I love my animals. I also track what I spend on them. Three pets, three sets of annual vaccines, three consultation fees every time something's off. It adds up fast.
"£33/month per pet. All of them. No surprises."
The proof
Two cats and a dog at £33 each is £99/month with unlimited visits and no bills. The same three pets on separate insurance plans, paying consultation fees on top, is easily twice that. The maths is the message. Show it clearly and let people do it themselves.
Channel first
Pet Facebook groups, Reddit (r/CatsUK, r/dogs), dog owner WhatsApp communities, neighbourhood apps. These are places people already compare pet costs openly.
