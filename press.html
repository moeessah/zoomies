Craft + acquisition

The photo brief is in the piece. It tells the journalist we've thought about the shoot. It also signals consent is in process.

Sarah says she can remember the exact evening she decided not to take Pip to the vet.

Her greyhound had been favouring his back left leg for a few days. He was still eating. Still asking for walks. Still leaning his long face into her hand on the sofa. "It wasn't dramatic," she says. "That was the problem. It was just enough to worry me, not enough to make the cost feel unavoidable."

Sarah is 34, works in UX design, rents in Hackney, and has the kind of spreadsheet many people use when they are trying to be sensible about money. Pip has his own row: food, insurance, walker, medication, extras. What the spreadsheet couldn't tell her was what a vet would find, and what that finding would cost.

She waited nine weeks.

Acquisition signal

"Nine weeks" is the paid social headline. It's specific enough to feel true. "A few months" isn't. NJ.com will lift this number too when we pitch the Clifton story.

When she finally went, Pip's leg turned out to be nothing serious. An old soft-tissue issue. Rest, monitoring, no drama. The bill was £190. "I cried in the car," Sarah says. "Not because of his leg. Because I had spent nine weeks feeling guilty and scared, and then I still had to pay nearly £200."

Creative asset

The £190 figure plus nine weeks does more work than any product headline. This is the paid social card.

The hidden cost of not knowing what vets cost

The Competition and Markets Authority's final report into veterinary services found that pet owners spent over £6.7 billion on veterinary services in 2024. The CMA documented a clear need for pricing transparency so that owners can compare practices before they're already under pressure.

The pressure is already changing behaviour. Research reported by Pet Gazette found that almost one in three British pet owners had delayed or skipped veterinary treatment because of rising costs. In the CMA's own qualitative research, owners described a particular bind: you love the animal, you know the vet is doing skilled work, but you still feel exposed when you can't see the cost before the decision is made.

Journalist note

Collinson cited this CMA figure in March. It's already in his mental model. Sarah doesn't confirm the stat. She makes it feel like something that happened to a real person.

It turns ordinary symptoms into private negotiations. Is the limp bad enough? Is the cough new? How much is the difference between "probably fine" and "I wish I had gone sooner"?

Owners are asked to make a financial decision and a moral decision at the same time, often in a room where someone has just said, "we can run a test."

Veterinary care is expensive to run. The difficulty is that owners discover exactly how expensive when the animal is sick and the decision is already half-made.

The CMA's proposed remedy package focuses on transparency: clearer ownership information, clearer price lists, easier comparison. Those changes may help at the point of choosing a practice. They don't solve the moment Sarah describes: when a pet is unwell, the owner is worried, and uncertainty itself is the barrier.

"The people affected aren't the ones who can't afford a dog. They're people who have the dog, love the dog, and are still doing the calculation about whether to call the vet."

Copy insight

The landing page argument lives here. £33/month doesn't sell as "save money on vet bills." It sells as: stop doing the calculation every time the dog seems off.

The model that's trying to fix it

Marcus Webb says he kept seeing the same gap from different corners of the pet industry. Owners who had insurance, had money, and still hesitated before calling the vet. If the cost of asking is predictable, people ask sooner. If they ask sooner, vets see more early signs. That was the bet.

Before Zoomies, he worked across pet technology and healthcare, including BARK and Pawp.

"Insurance can help with big shocks," he says. "But it doesn't always remove the hesitation around everyday care. People still ask themselves: if I go in, what will they find, and what will it cost?"

Pitch note

Journalists distrust founders who open with the product. Marcus's quote establishes what the membership doesn't cover before explaining what it does. That ordering earns the interview.

Zoomies currently operates six London clinics with around 6,000 members and an NPS above 70. It is opening its first US clinic in Clifton, New Jersey in the coming weeks, at $55 a month, into a market where the nearest comparable, Banfield, charges similar fees but covers considerably less.

Those numbers matter less than the behavioural change they point to. Traditional vet economics can make every appointment feel like a transaction. Membership economics tries to make contact routine. That difference changes when an owner picks up the phone.

Zoomies doesn't cover specialist referrals or catastrophic emergencies. Its claim is narrower: most of the hesitation lives in everyday symptoms, small worries, appointments you talk yourself out of. That's where contact frequency actually changes.

Sarah joined after Pip's delayed visit. She says he has been in three times since, and she has sent two remote messages for advice.

"I've never once thought about what it was going to cost before asking. That shouldn't be the luxury version of owning a dog. But right now, it is."

Distribution note

"If the cost of asking is predictable, owners ask sooner" belongs in the landing page hero, the investor narrative, and the onboarding email. One sentence, four formats.

The story angle

Why the Clifton story is different from the Guardian story

The Guardian piece is a systemic story. Sarah is the human entry point into a problem affecting 10 million British households.

The NJ.com piece is a local news story. A UK company chose Clifton, New Jersey, specifically, and the reporter wants to know why. That's the interview. Lead with Marcus's answer to that question.

The Banfield comparison is the sharpest angle. Banfield operates in the area at comparable monthly fees. Same price, considerably more included. That's the sentence the reporter lifts for their headline.

Why Clifton, specifically

The answer Marcus has to give

The reporter asks this in the first two minutes. The answer needs to be specific, not "we saw an opportunity in the US market."

  • Pet ownership density in Passaic County relative to vet practice coverage
  • Median household income in Clifton: high enough for $55/month, underserved by existing premium care
  • Commute proximity to Manhattan: a working population that values time as well as cost
  • Banfield presence: validates the market appetite, creates the comparison angle
  • Local group density on platforms like Nextdoor: one NJ.com article spreads via local social faster than any other US market
Finding the reporter

The method, not "search the masthead"

Go to NJ.com. Filter for Clifton or Passaic County stories from the last six months. Look at local business coverage, new openings, consumer stories.

If the same byline appears across vet pricing, local business, and healthcare access stories, start there. Then check LinkedIn before sending.

Do this research 12 days before opening. Build the email 10 days out. Pitch 8 days before. A local reporter needs a week minimum to arrange photography and get editorial sign-off.

What happens after the piece runs

Local articles behave differently

A Guardian piece drives direct traffic to the Zoomies website from high-intent readers. An NJ.com piece does something different: it seeds Nextdoor and local Facebook groups where Clifton pet owners actually make decisions.

The piece needs a direct path to a Clifton-specific landing page, not the homepage. "Read the story, see what your first month looks like" is the CTA. The QR code on the clinic handout points to the same place.

Monitor Nextdoor Clifton in the 72 hours after publication. If the article gets shared there, respond in the thread with Marcus's name and direct availability.

Why this post, not a press release

A press release says "Clifton opening was a success." A LinkedIn post from the founder that includes a real surprise from week one is actually interesting. Journalists who covered the launch will see it. Investors will see it. Potential members in Clifton will see it.

The trade press angle

The learning post can also be repurposed into a short op-ed for Vet Times or a veterinary podcast: "What happens to member visit frequency in the first 30 days of a US membership clinic." That's a story the veterinary industry doesn't have data on yet. We do.

The acquisition mechanism

The last two lines of the post, Clifton pet owner, thinking about joining, here's the address, are the call to action. Framed inside an honest operational post rather than a marketing message. Someone reading a founder's ops post is in a different headspace than someone seeing an ad. The same ask lands differently.

What makes this press, not content

If the post gets picked up by NJ.com as a follow-up to their opening piece ("Six weeks in: what the Zoomies founder learned"), that's the third piece in the campaign. It also gives the next investor update a data point that's hard to fabricate: real visit frequency numbers from a live US clinic in its first fortnight.

What's not in the plan
Six things deliberately left out.

Getting the wrong coverage at this stage sets brand associations before enough members exist to correct them.

  • ×
    Mail Online

    The reach is real. The audience overlap with Zoomies members is low, and the format, viral animal stories, outrage pieces, creates associations that are hard to undo.

  • ×
    Contributed content and sponsored editorial

    If Zoomies wrote it, it's not press. Sponsored placements at this stage signal to investors and journalists that we couldn't earn the coverage.

  • ×
    Reactive quotes in roundup pieces

    A 100-word quote in a "10 experts on rising vet costs" roundup drives no member acquisition and weak search upside. The time spent arranging it is time not spent on the NJ.com pitch.

  • ×
    Trade press as a primary hit

    Vet Times and Pet Business World are secondary at best. The people reading them already know veterinary care costs money. The audience who needs to discover Zoomies isn't reading trade publications.

  • ×
    Any pitch that frames Zoomies as "like pet insurance"

    Once a journalist writes that comparison, it follows the brand. The product is not insurance. If a pitch angle requires that framing to make sense, we're pitching the wrong angle. Better to not get the coverage.

  • ×
    Cold email blasts with the press release

    A press release exists to give a journalist a fact sheet after they've already agreed to write the story. Sending it cold is how you get filtered as PR noise. The release skeleton belongs in the pitch pack, not in the outgoing email.
